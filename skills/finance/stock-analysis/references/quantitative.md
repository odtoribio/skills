# Quantitative criteria (the numbers)

The quantitative analysis answers three questions in order: **Does it really make money?**, **Is it financially solid?**, and **Is it fairly priced?** Every criterion below belongs to one of those three blocks.

No single indicator is read in isolation — they validate each other. A stock that only passes valuation (cheap) can be a value trap; one that only passes profitability can be overvalued. The goal is a company that passes all three blocks at once.

**Thresholds are sector-relative.** Debt/Equity, ROE, and P/E especially depend on the sector. Banks, utilities, and capital-intensive industrials run healthy at leverage that would alarm a software company. Always compare against same-sector peers, not a universal absolute number. Identify the sector and 3–5 comparable peers before scoring.

Score each criterion as **PASS / PARTIAL / FAIL / UNVERIFIED** (use UNVERIFIED when data couldn't be confirmed — never guess). Prefer ~10 years of history (or as much as is available) so you can judge consistency, not a single good year.

## Block 1 · Does it make money? (Profitability & growth)

**1. Revenue** — *Essential*
Measures the top line, the base of everything. Quality matters (organic vs. acquisition-driven growth) and so does consistency.
PASS when: rising and stable over the years. Steady ~5–10%/yr is usually preferable to a 40% spike followed by a fall. Stagnant or declining revenue → FAIL.

**2. Net income** — *Essential*
Confirms the company is profitable, not just selling. Can be distorted by one-off accounting events (asset sales, tax adjustments), so cross-check against cash flow.
PASS when: positive and growing steadily, ideally at a rate ≥ revenue growth (sign of improving efficiency).

**3. Operating cash flow** — *Essential*
The most honest profitability metric — the real cash the business generates, hard to manipulate.
PASS when: positive and growing, ideally ≥ net income (shows accounting earnings convert to real cash).

**4. Return on Equity (ROE)** — *Essential*
Profit generated per unit of shareholder capital. A high ROE can be artificial when driven by heavy debt (debt shrinks equity and inflates ROE), so always read alongside Debt/Equity.
PASS when: > 15%, sustained over time (not one good year).

**5. Margins — gross, operating, net** — *Recommended*
Measure efficiency and pricing power. High, stable margins often signal a competitive advantage (a moat). They explain *why* the company is or isn't profitable.
PASS when: stable or rising over time and above direct peers. Eroding margins → warning.

**6. Free cash flow (FCF)** — *Recommended*
Cash left after capital expenditures — the money actually available for shareholders (dividends, buybacks, debt reduction). Many consider it the single most important metric.
PASS when: positive and growing, ideally FCF conversion (FCF ÷ net income) at or above 1.

**7. Earnings per share (EPS)** — *Recommended*
Translates net income to what each share actually gets. Neutralizes share-issuance dilution that total net income hides.
PASS when: sustained EPS growth. If net income grows but EPS doesn't, there's dilution → warning.

**8. Return on Invested Capital (ROIC)** — *Recommended*
Superior to ROE because it isn't distorted by debt — it measures return on all invested capital (equity + debt). Many professionals prefer it as the true quality indicator.
PASS when: > 10% sustained, ideally above the company's cost of capital (WACC).

## Block 2 · Is it solid? (Financial strength)

**9. Total assets vs. total liabilities** — *Essential*
Verifies positive net worth (the company is worth more than it owes). A minimum filter — almost any healthy company passes, so on its own it doesn't discriminate much.
PASS when: assets > liabilities; better still if the gap (equity) grows year over year.

**10. Debt / Equity** — *Essential*
Leverage — how much debt vs. own capital. Heavy debt amplifies risk in bad times. Threshold is strongly sector-dependent.
PASS when: < 1 as a general rule. **Exception:** banks, utilities, and capital-intensive industrials run healthy at D/E of 2–3. Compare against same-sector peers.

**11. Current ratio** — *Essential*
Short-term liquidity — can the company cover obligations due within a year using current assets.
PASS when: > 1 (ideally 1.5–3). A very high value (>3) can mean idle cash that isn't being put to work.

**12. Interest coverage ratio** — *Recommended*
Whether operating earnings (EBIT) comfortably cover debt interest. Complements Debt/Equity: a company can carry high debt yet handle it well if it earns enough.
PASS when: > 3 (EBIT covers interest at least 3×). Below 1.5 → serious financial risk.

**13. Shares outstanding** — *Recommended*
Detects dilution. Heavy new-share issuance shrinks your stake even if the company grows. Often overlooked.
PASS when: stable or declining (buybacks are usually positive). A strong, steady increase → warning.

---

## Block 3 · Is it fairly priced? (Valuation)

**14. P/E ratio** — *Essential*
What the market pays per unit of earnings. Meaningful only in comparison — a high-growth company "deserves" a higher P/E than a mature one.
PASS when: similar to or below direct sector peers, and below its own historical average.

**15. PEG ratio** — *Essential*
Corrects P/E's main weakness by dividing it by expected growth. Depends on an uncertain growth estimate — treat as a guide, not gospel.
Reading: = 1 fair · < 1 cheap (attractive) · > 1 expensive. PASS when ≤ 1.

**16. Intrinsic value vs. market price** — *Essential*
The heart of value investing — buy for less than something is worth. Calculating intrinsic value (usually via a DCF) takes many assumptions and two analysts can land far apart, so state your assumptions plainly.
PASS when: intrinsic value > market price, ideally with a 20–30% margin of safety (paying 70–80¢ per dollar of estimated value).

## Decision rule (quantitative)

**Non-negotiable:** the three essential solidity criteria (9, 10, 11) must pass. A company that fails here is discarded regardless of everything else — a profitable, cheap company that could go bankrupt is not a good investment.

**General threshold:** at least **12 of 16** criteria pass (~75%), with minimum coverage in every block: ≥ 5 of 8 in Profitability, all 3 essentials in Solidity, ≥ 2 of 3 in Valuation.

| Points passed | Reading | Suggested action |
|---|---|---|
| 14–16 / 16 | High-quality company at a good price | Strong buy candidate (still verify the qualitative side) |
| 12–13 / 16 | Solid, with a point or two to watch | Buyable if the misses aren't in solidity and you understand why they fail |
| 9–11 / 16 | Mixed signals | Watch / research more before deciding. Don't buy yet |
| < 9 / 16 | Weak in one or more blocks | Discard or wait for improvement |

**The number doesn't replace judgment.** Two valuation misses (expensive stock) mean something very different from two solidity misses (bankruptcy risk). Weigh severity, not just count — a single grave solidity failure can outweigh three minor ones spread around.
