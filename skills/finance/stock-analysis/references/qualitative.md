# Qualitative criteria (the business behind the numbers)

The quantitative side tells you *what* the company has done. It doesn't tell you *why*, or whether it will keep doing it. That's the qualitative side. Numbers are a photo of the past; the qualitative analysis is what helps you estimate the future.

Unlike ratios, qualitative factors rarely reduce to a numeric threshold ("must be > X"). They're judgments about the business. So instead of a parameter, each point lists **favorable signals** and **warning signals** — read the balance between them. Score each as **FAVORABLE / MIXED / WARNING / UNKNOWN**.

---

## Block A · The competitive advantage (the "moat")

The most important concept in qualitative analysis, popularized by Warren Buffett. A moat is what protects the company's profits from competition. Without one, good numbers attract competitors who erode them.

**1. Is there a durable competitive advantage?**
Why: it's what lets a company keep high margins and profitability for years. Without a moat, competitors copy the product and cut prices until no one makes money.
Key question: what stops a well-funded competitor from taking the business tomorrow?
Favorable: high, stable margins over years (the quantitative fingerprint of a moat); customers who don't easily leave; ability to raise prices without losing sales.
Warning: easily replicable product; competition purely on price; steadily eroding margins.

**2. What kind of moat is it? (the 5 sources)**
Why: identifying the source tells you how durable it is and what could destroy it. Not all moats are equally deep.
The five classic types:
- **Intangible assets:** strong brands, patents, licenses (e.g. Coca-Cola, pharma).
- **Switching costs:** costly or painful for the customer to switch (e.g. enterprise software, banks).
- **Network effect:** the service improves as more users join (e.g. social networks, card networks).
- **Cost advantage:** produces cheaper than anyone (e.g. scale, location, unique processes).
- **Efficient scale:** the market only supports a few profitable players (e.g. utilities, airports).
Favorable: one or more types present and reinforcing over time.
Warning: fits none, or the advantage rests on a single fragile factor (one expiring patent, one contract).

## Block B · Management

A company can have a great business and still destroy value if run badly.

**3. Do they allocate capital well?**
Why: management's #1 job is deciding what to do with earnings (reinvest, pay down debt, dividends, buybacks, acquisitions). Good decisions compound value; bad ones burn it.
Key question: does reinvested money earn good returns (high, sustained ROIC)? Do they buy companies at sensible prices or out of ego?
Favorable: track record of profitable investment, buybacks when the stock is cheap, disciplined acquisitions.
Warning: expensive value-destroying acquisitions, buybacks when the stock is expensive, "empire-building" for prestige over returns.

**4. Are they honest and transparent?**
Why: financial reports depend on the integrity of whoever signs them. Management that massages figures or hides bad news is a huge risk to minority investors.
Key question: do they own their mistakes in shareholder letters, or only tout wins and blame the environment?
Favorable: clear, consistent communication; admits errors; conservative accounting; meets the targets it sets.
Warning: vague or over-optimistic language; frequent accounting changes; recurring "adjustments" that always flatter; high turnover of auditors or CFOs.

**5. Are they aligned with shareholders?**
Why: if management's interests match yours, good long-term decisions are more likely. If not, they may prioritize a quarterly bonus over your investment.
Key question: do executives have meaningful personal wealth in the company's stock ("skin in the game")?
Favorable: executives/founders with relevant equity stakes, compensation tied to long-term metrics, reasonable salaries.
Warning: executives with no stock of their own, outsized pay disconnected from performance, constant insider selling, aggressive dilution.

## Block C · The sector and the business

A company doesn't operate in a vacuum. Its sector shapes much of its fate regardless of how well it's run.

**6. Is the sector growing, stable, or declining?**
Why: it's far easier to thrive in a growing sector. An excellent company in a declining sector swims against the current.
Key question: where will this sector be in 10 years? Will demand grow or be replaced by another technology?
Favorable: structurally rising demand; long-term tailwinds (demographics, technology, regulation).
Warning: mature or shrinking sector; threat of tech disruption; dependence on a passing fad.

**7. How intense is the competition?**
Why: competitive structure (Porter's 5 forces) determines how much profit is possible. A price-war sector leaves little for anyone.
Key question: does it compete with a few rational rivals, or in a fragmented market where everyone cannibalizes everyone?
Favorable: few large, rational competitors; high barriers to entry; bargaining power over customers and suppliers.
Warning: easy entry for new competitors; commoditized products; suppliers or customers holding all the bargaining power.

**8. Do you understand how it makes money? (circle of competence)**
Why: if you can't explain the business model in a sentence or two, you can't assess its risks. Buffett calls this investing within your "circle of competence."
Key question: could you explain to someone, simply, where the revenue comes from and why it will keep coming?
Favorable: simple, understandable model; recurring, predictable revenue sources.
Warning: a model so complex the company itself explains it poorly; unpredictable or one-off revenue.

## Block D · Specific risks

Beyond the sector, every company has its own vulnerabilities that average numbers hide.

**9. Is there dangerous concentration?**
Why: over-dependence on one customer, product, supplier, or region turns a single problem into an existential crisis.
Key question: what happens if the largest customer leaves, or the flagship product stops selling?
Favorable: diversified base of customers, products, and geographies; no single leg holding up the business.
Warning: one customer is a large share of sales; one product drives almost all profit; reliance on a single supplier or country.

**10. What regulatory, legal, or external risks does it face?**
Why: regulation, litigation, political risk, or technological change can alter a company's value overnight without showing up in historical ratios.
Key question: what external event, outside the company's control, could seriously harm it?
Favorable: stable regulatory environment; no major pending litigation; low geopolitical exposure.
Warning: sector under rising regulatory pressure; significant open litigation; dependence on subsidies or a legal framework that could change.

## Summary checklist

| # | Point | Favorable signal | Warning signal |
|---|-------|-----------------|----------------|
| 1 | Durable advantage | High, stable margins | Easy to copy |
| 2 | Type of moat | One+ solid types | Single, fragile advantage |
| 3 | Capital allocation | Profitable investments | Expensive acquisitions |
| 4 | Management honesty | Admits errors, clarity | Vague language, constant adjustments |
| 5 | Shareholder alignment | Executives own stock | Outsized compensation |
| 6 | Sector health | Growing demand | Declining or disrupted sector |
| 7 | Competitive intensity | Few rivals, high barriers | Easy entry, price war |
| 8 | Understandable business | Simple, recurring model | Too complex or unpredictable |
| 9 | Risk concentration | Diversified base | Dependence on one customer/product |
| 10 | External risks | Stable environment | Regulatory pressure or litigation |

## Combining quantitative + qualitative

The final verdict comes from joining both halves: **business quality (qualitative) × price & numbers (quantitative).**

| | Solid numbers / good price | Weak numbers / expensive |
|---|---|---|
| **Excellent business** (strong moat, good management) | **Ideal buy.** Great company at a good price — the target scenario. | **Wait.** Great company but expensive. Watch it and buy on a correction. |
| **Weak business** (no moat, high risk) | **Value trap.** Cheap for a reason; the numbers may deteriorate soon. Avoid. | **Discard.** Bad company and expensive. No appeal. |

**The qualitative side has veto power.** A company can pass all 16 quantitative criteria, but if you don't understand the business, management is dishonest, or there's no competitive advantage, those good numbers probably won't last. As Buffett put it, it's far better to buy a wonderful company at a fair price than a fair company at a wonderful price.

**Don't seek perfection, seek understanding.** Almost no company ticks every qualitative box. The goal isn't the perfect investment — it's understanding what you're buying and what could go wrong. A risk you know and accept is very different from one that blindsides you.
